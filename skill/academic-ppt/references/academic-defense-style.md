# Academic Defense Style

## Core Purpose

The deck is a defense artifact, not a condensed thesis manuscript.

It should help the committee quickly judge:
- what problem was studied
- why the problem matters
- what the student actually did
- whether the method is logical
- whether the evidence is credible
- where the innovation is
- what the limits are

## Content Priorities

- Prioritize the student's own work over broad background.
- Keep the narrative closed-loop: problem -> method -> validation -> conclusion.
- Make innovation visible both in a dedicated summary slide and in the relevant method/result slides.
- Distinguish clearly between prior work, borrowed components, and original contribution.
- Prefer fewer but stronger result slides over a complete transfer of all thesis figures.

## Language Rules

- Use professional, restrained, conclusion-oriented language.
- Prefer phrases and short clauses over paragraphs.
- Avoid promotional or exaggerated wording.
- Keep terminology stable across slides.
- Use slide titles that carry meaning. Prefer conclusion titles over empty labels like "Results" or "Analysis".

## Figure And Chart Rules

- Every major figure or chart must answer a specific question.
- Every major figure or chart must be accompanied by a short conclusion statement.
- Explain axes, comparison objects, and what the main pattern means.
- Do not paste dense paper figures unchanged when they are too hard to explain in under one minute.
- Split, crop, redraw, or simplify complex paper figures for slide use.
- Cite external figures or reused visuals directly on the slide.

## Visual Rules

- One main idea per slide.
- Favor readability from a projected defense room over decorative complexity.
- Keep typography large, contrast high, and backgrounds quiet.
- Keep visible editable text at or above `18px` whenever possible.
- Use color consistently and do not rely on color alone to encode meaning.
- Use no more than a small, controlled palette unless the source template requires otherwise.
- Keep school branding visually secondary to the thesis claim and evidence.
- Prefer 2 to 3 evidence bullets on a content page instead of filling the page with text blocks.
- Let method and result pages give real space to figures, diagrams, or charts instead of treating visuals as decoration.

## Defense-Oriented Checks

Each major section should help answer likely committee questions:
- why this problem
- why this method
- why these experiments
- why the result is trustworthy
- why the innovation claim is justified
- what the work cannot yet do

## Prohibited Outcomes

- Background-heavy decks with little evidence of the student's work
- Paragraph blocks copied from the thesis
- Unexplained figures
- Result claims without baselines or setup context
- Innovation claims stated only as adjectives
- Slides so dense that the speaker must read them aloud
